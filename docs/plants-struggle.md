# Why Plants Struggle

A wastewater treatment plant normally has a known hydrautic limit, but the solids limit is often more obscure.  The relevant design basis may be old, distributed across several reports, or tied to multiple interacting processes.

Water conservation has reduced hydrauling loads to WWTPs while solids just keep increasing. Over time this may push plants to solids overload before they are at the hydraulic limit.

## Operators often see symptoms first

Even when no one can identify a single legal or design threshold, plant staff may observe:

- increasing mixed-liquor or sludge inventories;
- clarifiers operating with less reserve capacity;
- digesters or sludge storage remaining full;
- increasing aeration demand;
- worsening dewatering performance;
- more polymer use;
- more frequent hauling;
- difficulty maintaining performance during peak loads.

These symptoms do not prove that the entire plant has reached a defined solids limit. They do show that solids loading may be reducing operational flexibility.

## Why solids loads are increasing

Water conservation can reduce per-capita wastewater flow while the amount of toilet paper, lint, fecal solids and other solids remains similar or increases with population. The result is faster rising solids loading compared to the hydraulic loading.

## The FECR response

A microscreen installed near the front of the plant can remove a large fraction of incoming suspended solids before those solids load biological treatment and back-end solids handling.
